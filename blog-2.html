Divorce Fault Grounds Overview

Home News Divorce Fault Grounds Overview

FAULT

Every year, at least one bill is introduced to the Mississippi Legislature which would allow a spouse to obtain a divorce without having to obtain the consent of the other spouse, and without having to prove “fault grounds.” As of this writing, the Legislature has never passed such a bill. So the divorce law in Mississippi remains, that to obtain a divorce without the other spouse’s consent, a party has to prove at least one “fault ground.”

----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WHAT ARE THE FAULT GROUNDS FOR DIVORCE IN MISSISSIPPI?

----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The fault grounds are sorted into groups of general misconduct (drug and alcohol abuse, desertion, cruelty, being imprisoned in the penitentiary), sexual issues (adultery or the wife’s pregnancy at the time of the marriage), health issues (impotence, mental illness, mental retardation), and finally, bigamy and incest. Here is the full list of available fault grounds.

Natural Impotency Mental Illness/Retardation at the Time of Marriage
Adultery Bigamy
Being Sentenced to a Penitentiary Wife’s Unknown Pregnancy by Other Man at the time of Marriage
Desertion for 1 Year Incurable Mental Illness
Habitual Drunkenness
Habitual Use of Heroin, Etc.
Habitual Cruel & Inhuman Treatment

Not included as a fault ground is a failed marriage. Rudeness, unkindness and incompatibility will not suffice. Even where both spouses agree that their marriage is a failure and that neither wants to be married, if they nevertheless cannot agree to a no-fault divorce, one spouse will have to prove a fault ground.

HOW MUCH PROOF DO YOU NEED FOR A FAULT-BASED DIVORCE

There are two general standards for evidence in civil cases (this is everywhere, not just in Mississippi).

Preponderance of the Evidence means proof that is at least enough to tip the scales in your favor. Think of it as proof that is at least 51% convincing, as opposed to the opponent’s proof being only 49% convincing or less. The judge isn’t necessarily convinced by your evidence, but he finds it to be at least a little more persuasive than the other side’s evidence.

Clear and Convincing Evidence means proof that a particular fact is substantially more likely than not to be true. In practical terms, it means that the judge (or jury) must be pretty well convinced that a factual proposition is true, even though there is still some possibility that it is not true.

Proof Beyond a Reasonable Doubt is used in criminal cases, but does not apply to divorce cases in Mississippi.

The standard of evidence that applies depends on which fault ground you are trying to prove. Here’s the chart:

Preponderance of the Evidence Clear and Convincing Evidence
Habitual Cruel & Inhuman Treatment All Other Fault Grounds

CAN’T WE JUST AGREE TO A FAULT GROUND

(OR AT LEAST AGREE TO NOT FIGHT ABOUT IT)

Even if the defendant/spouse does not contest the fault ground, the plaintiff/spouse must still go to court and prove its existence. While parties are prohibited from colluding to falsely allege fault grounds that in fact never took place, they can prove the existence of fault grounds that did take place by admitting to them in court. What’s the difference? In the former, the parties agree to lie about misconduct that never occurred. In the latter, the parties agree to tell the truth about misconduct that did occur.

----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

WHAT IS CORROBORATING EVIDENCE

No matter how convincing a spouse’s testimony in support of a fault ground, without more evidence, that spouse’s testimony, standing alone, is never enough. That testimony must be corroborated by other evidence. Some examples of corroborating evidence might be:

Testimony from another witness, such as a friend or a private investigator

Admission by the defendant/spouse

Photographs/Videos/Audio Recordings

Social Media Posts

Medical Records

Court Records

Expert witness testimony

The corroborating evidence does not have to prove the entirety of the claimed fault ground, but it must be enough to help convince the judge that the fault ground is true, rather than nothing more than exaggerations of a spouse trying to get a divorce. A witness is not providing corroborating evidence if all he does is repeat what the plaintiff/spouse told him.
